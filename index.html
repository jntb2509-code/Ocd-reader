Chapter 1

Diagnosis, Phenomenology, and Comorbidity

OCD has long been one of the most puzzling disorders in clinical psychology — a paradox of insight and helplessness. This chapter covers the diagnostic criteria, epidemiology, phenomenology, and comorbid conditions that shape our understanding of the disorder.

The OCD Paradox

Individuals with OCD are tormented by repetitive thoughts about dreaded possibilities they recognize as exaggerated — yet feel helpless to stop carrying out stereotypic rituals. This creates a self-perpetuating cycle:

Case Study — Louise A 37-year-old mother whose contamination fear began at age 14 following a lice outbreak. Now obsessed with contracting cancer through contact with objects others have touched. Scrubs hands until they bleed, carries antibacterial wipes everywhere. Despite medication and conventional counseling, symptoms continue unabated — eventually leading to comorbid depression and suicidal ideation.

DSM-5 Diagnosis of OCD

The essential features of OCD are the repeated occurrence of personally distressing or functionally impairing obsessions and/or compulsions.

Core DSM-5 Definitions
Obsessions
Unwanted, unacceptable, and repetitive intrusive thoughts, images, or urges that are resisted and difficult to control. Content often focuses on contamination, aggression, doubt, unacceptable sexual acts, religion, or symmetry.
Compulsions
Repetitive behaviors or mental acts associated with subjective urgency, aimed at preventing a dreaded outcome or reducing distress caused by an obsession. Generally accompanied by a strong urge and diminished sense of voluntary control.

The Reclassification Debate

DSM-5 moved OCD from the Anxiety Disorders category to a new "Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders" category, alongside BDD, hoarding disorder, trichotillomania, and excoriation disorder. This was highly controversial.

Arguments FOR Reclassification

  • Symptom similarity with BDD and hoarding disorder
  • Common core of repetitive/compulsive behavior on a continuum with impulsivity
  • Similar clinical course, age of onset, and family history
  • Shared frontal-striatal neural circuitry (vs. amygdala in anxiety disorders)
  • Similar treatment response to SSRIs

Arguments AGAINST Reclassification

  • "Compulsivity" as core feature ignores the anxiety-relief function of rituals
  • Little empirical evidence impulsivity and compulsivity lie on same continuum
  • Repetitive behavior appears in many disorders — lacks diagnostic specificity
  • Higher comorbidity with some anxiety disorders than with spectrum disorders
  • ERP works for OCD but not for TTM or excoriation disorder
  • Neural circuitry evidence is inconsistent
Author's Position Clark maintains that OCD is best understood as an anxiety disorder. The basic DSM-5 diagnostic criteria can be accepted without agreeing to its diagnostic segregation.

Family Involvement

Family members often make accommodations for the person's rituals, which increases family stress and dysfunction. A meta-analysis found greater OCD symptom severity is associated with more family accommodation — regardless of gender, age, or comorbidity. This creates a vicious cycle: accommodation → symptom escalation → more accommodation.

Quality of Life and Suicidality

OCD has a significant negative impact on social and occupational functioning. Individuals with OCD show significantly lower QOL scores in work, social, emotional, and family domains compared to healthy controls.

Suicidality Risk Two large community studies found that 36–63% of individuals with OCD reported suicidal thoughts at some point during their life, and 11–26% reported lifetime suicide attempts. Presence of sexual/religious obsessions and comorbid major depression increase suicidal risk. Clinicians must assess suicidal risk — especially in severe OCD comorbid with depression.

Course and Outcome

Treatment Seeking

Most individuals with OCD delay seeking treatment for 2 to 7 years (median: 9 years in one study). Those with milder symptoms are less likely to seek treatment. Even when treatment utilization is high, less than one-third of individuals with severe OCD receive treatment specifically for OCD.

The low level of treatment seeking reflects a broader dissemination problem: even those with severe OCD may not recognize that disorder-specific treatment is needed.

Natural Course OCD tends to be a chronic, waxing-and-waning disorder. Rates may decline with age. The average length of the disorder is about 8¾ years (NCS-R). A substantial number experience gradual onset, while for others onset is acute, often in response to critical life events.
Chapter 2

Obsessions, Intrusions, and Their Correlates

What distinguishes a clinical obsession from ordinary unwanted thoughts? This chapter explores the nature of obsessional thinking — its content, core features, and relationship to normal intrusive thoughts — and how to differentiate obsessions from worry and rumination.

Obsessional Content

Most individuals with OCD experience multiple obsessions (50–99% across studies). Obsessional content is highly individualistic, shaped by personal experience, culture, and critical life events. The primary obsessional content can also change over time.

Cultural Influences

While contamination, harm/aggression, and pathological doubt are universal across cultures, there are significant differences in other types. Religious obsessions and concerns about impurity are far more common in Eastern religious cultures. Multinational studies across 11 countries show the largest cross-cultural differences involve sex, religion, and harm intrusions.

Trauma-Related Obsessions

Life experiences, especially severe negative events or traumas, can influence the content of obsessional ideation and onset of OCD. One review concluded that "the impact of trauma on OCD is irrefutable" (Dykshoorn, 2014), though the etiological significance of environmental factors remains uncertain.

Core Features of Obsessions

Contemporary definitions emphasize four core features. None is necessary or sufficient alone, but together they identify the obsessive quality of repetitive thought:

1

Intrusiveness

The thought, image, or impulse repeatedly enters consciousness in an unintended, involuntary manner — against one's will. Even when an obsession can be tolerated when generated voluntarily in therapy, the same obsession is experienced as intolerable when it appears as a sudden mental intrusion in daily life.

2

Unacceptability

The intrusion is considered unwanted, undesired, or morally disapproved. An intrusion may be unacceptable because it: (1) threatens core values, (2) diminishes self-worth, or (3) is associated with high subjective distress. Appraisals of low probability but high disapproval are associated with greater use of maladaptive escape/avoidance strategies.

3

Subjective Resistance

A strong urge to ignore, suppress, or neutralize the obsession through avoidance, mental control strategies, or compulsive rituals. The desire to rid one's mind of the obsession is fueled by the belief that undesirable consequences will occur if not successfully terminated.

4

Perceived Uncontrollability

An evaluation of diminished control over the obsession that is considered unacceptable and threatening. Despite strong motivation to resist, individuals with OCD perceive their efforts at control as short-lived and inadequate. Notably, research suggests individuals with OCD do not exhibit an actual decrement in control ability — the critical feature is the perception of uncontrollability.

Normal vs. Abnormal Obsessions

In a landmark 1978 study, Rachman and de Silva found that 84% of nonclinical participants reported unwanted cognitive intrusions qualitatively similar in form and content to clinical obsessions. This challenged the categorical view of OCD.

The Continuity Hypothesis CBT models adhere to the view that clinical and nonclinical differences in obsessive-compulsive symptoms are a matter of degree rather than kind. Normal intrusive thoughts develop into pathological obsessions when individuals misinterpret them as a significant personal threat requiring neutralization (Rachman, 2003; Salkovskis, 1985).

Chapter 3

Compulsions, Neutralization, and Control

Any response to an obsession can become a compulsion if it occurs repeatedly to neutralize the obsession, reduce distress, or prevent its feared outcome. This chapter explores behavioral and mental compulsions, reassurance seeking, avoidance, neutralization, and thought suppression.

Behavioral Compulsions

Compulsions are "perseverative, repetitive actions that are excessive and inappropriate to a situation" (Berlin & Hollander, 2014). They can range from higher-order cognitive responses (praying, mentally rehearsing) to simple motor responses (tapping, checking, washing).

Case Study — Charles A fastidious man with compulsive tidying behavior. He had a strong urge to arrange anything that looked messy or out of place and couldn't relax until everything felt "just right." His behavior met multiple criteria for clinical compulsions: (1) strong urge to engage, (2) neutralization function ("just right" feeling), (3) varying insight, (4) highly repetitive and stereotypic pattern, (5) impaired response inhibition, and (6) cognitive inflexibility.

Defining Features of Clinical Compulsions

●

Harm Avoidance (not Reward)

The essence of a clinical compulsion is the intense urge to perform the response, which serves a harm avoidance function — in contrast to the reward-seeking function in impulse disorders like kleptomania or pathological gambling.

●

Functional Relationship with Obsessions

Obsessions and compulsions are functionally related in most cases — the obsession elicits anxiety, which the compulsion temporarily relieves. Factor-analytic studies confirm they load on the same factors.

●

Insight and Resistance

Insight may provoke subjective resistance so that the individual delays the compulsion — but eventually the urge becomes so strong the person gives in. Many individuals eventually abandon resistance, showing only slight or no attempt to resist.

Mental Compulsions

Mental compulsions (also called covert compulsions or cognitive neutralization) are internal, repetitive responses triggered by an obsession, with a strongly felt urge to think through some aspect of the obsession.

Mental Repetition

Repeating specific words, images, or numbers internally to "cancel" the obsession

Prayer / Phrases

Repetition of special prayers, songs, or protective phrases

Mental Counting

Counting to specific numbers or in specific sequences

Mental List Making

Compulsively reviewing lists of items or memories

Mental Reviewing

Questioning, analyzing, or mentally replaying events or decisions

Prevalence of Mental Compulsions In a sample of 225 adults with OCD, 12.9% reported mental rituals as their primary compulsion, and 20.4% as a current symptom. Only 50.7% reported no history of mental rituals. A multicenter Brazilian study found 56.7% reported a mental compulsion.

Excessive Reassurance Seeking (ERS)

ERS is a common response strategy found with distressing obsessions (41% of one OCD sample used direct reassurance seeking). Rachman (2002) described ERS as a "checking compulsion by proxy" — repeatedly seeking the same reassurance from others, looking for the same specific answer to obtain relief from anxiety and heightened responsibility.

Clinical Example — ERS in Action A middle-aged man with a sexual obsession repeatedly asked his wife whether sex with a former boyfriend (from 15 years prior) was better than with him. His interrogations were lengthy, accusatory, and often brought his wife to tears. Rarely did her response produce more than a fleeting sense of relief — so the questioning continued until she had to escape. The obsession was recognized as absurd, yet the urge was irresistible.

Key Characteristics of ERS in OCD

  1. ERS produces only transient relief from anxiety — distress returns and the urge to seek reassurance increases
  2. Individuals seek a specific response, even if vaguely articulated, that they believe will diminish the obsessional concern
  3. The compulsive urge associated with ERS can be as strong as any compulsion
  4. ERS has a significant negative emotional effect on family, friends, and caregivers from whom it is sought
Treatment Implication ERS must be included in the cognitive case conceptualization and targeted for treatment. Family members may need coaching. Therapists must be vigilant for subtle efforts to extract reassurance in the therapy session itself.

Avoidance

Avoidance involves any effort intended to avert a perceived trigger of the obsession and its associated distress. It is often the first choice of action individuals use to manage their OCD symptoms.

Avoidance contributes to the persistence of OCD in two ways:

1

Prevents Habituation

By preventing prolonged exposure to obsessional fears, avoidance prevents the natural anxiety reduction process from occurring.

2

Blocks Disconfirming Evidence

Avoidance is a safety behavior that deprives individuals of the opportunity to experience evidence against the threatening nature of the obsession (Salkovskis, 1996).

Neutralization

Neutralization is "any voluntary, effortful cognitive or behavioral act that is directed at removing, preventing, or attenuating the thought or the associated discomfort" (Freeston & Ladouceur, 1997). It includes but extends beyond compulsions.

Why Neutralization Maintains OCD

Experimental evidence confirms that neutralization causes an immediate reduction in anxiety — but a greater return of anxiety and stronger urge to neutralize occurs after a 30-minute delay. The temporary relief reinforces the compulsive cycle and enhances the perceived significance of the obsession.

The Paradox of Neutralization Although many neutralization strategies appear adaptive, they are problematic because their aim is to counter the negative effects of the obsession — thereby enhancing its meaning and significance. This is why even seemingly "reasonable" coping strategies can perpetuate OCD.

Thought Suppression and Mental Control

A significant area of research has focused on deliberate attempts to suppress unwanted thoughts. Wegner's classic "white bear" paradigm (1989) demonstrated a paradoxical effect: deliberate suppression of a target thought can lead to a subsequent increase in its frequency.

The Ironic Process Model of Thought Suppression
Deliberate Suppression "Don't think about it"
→
Operating Process Monitors for the thought
→
Ironic Effect Thought becomes more accessible
→
Rebound Increased thought frequency post-suppression

Individuals with OCD place excessive importance on controlling their thoughts and believe that failure to control an unwanted thought has serious consequences. This inflated importance of mental control is itself a cognitive appraisal that maintains the disorder.

Clinical Implication Rather than helping clients suppress obsessions, CBT aims to change the meaning attributed to intrusive thoughts and reduce the perceived need for mental control. The goal is not thought elimination but changed appraisal of the thoughts.
